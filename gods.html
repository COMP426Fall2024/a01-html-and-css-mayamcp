Mayan Gods and Goddesses of Note

Like many Mesoamerican indigenous groups, the Maya were polytheistic, meaning they believed in multiple gods/goddesses. Here are some of the most significant or interesting that are suspected to have existed. However, as with a lot of pre-colonial Indigenous history, a lot of this information is incomplete and potentially incorrect.

  • Q'uq'umatz: Similar to the Aztec god Quetzalcoatl, Q'uq'umatz is depicted as a feathered serpent. It is associated with wind, rain, water, clouds, and the sky.
  • Chaac: The god of rain, thunder, and lightning, Chaac wields a lightning axe which he uses to strike the clouds, causing rain and thunder.
  • Itzamna: Itzamna is a major deity associated with creation, wisdom, and the sky. He is often depicted as an elderly figure and is credited with inventing writing, the calendar, and medicine.
  • Ixchel: Ixchel is the goddess of medicine and childbirth. Often portrayed as an aged woman with a serpent crown, she is both a nurturing and powerful figure, overseeing the cycles of life, death, and the healing arts.
  • Yum Kaax: Yum Kaax is the god of wild vegetation, agriculture, and the forest. He is the protector of farmers and hunters, ensuring the growth of crops and the abundance of the natural world.
  • Ah-Muzen-Cab: Ah-Muzen-Cab is the god of bees and honey. He is a patron of beekeepers and is essential in the protection and cultivation of bees, which were sacred in Mayan culture.
  • Cizin: Cizin is a god of death and the underworld. He is often depicted as a skeletal being, associated with earthquakes and the afterlife, reminding the Maya of the inevitability of death.
  • Howler Monkey God: The Howler Monkey God is a patron of the arts, particularly music, writing, and sculpture. This deity, often depicted as a howler monkey, embodies creativity and skill, protecting artisans and their craft.
  • Kʼawiil: Kʼawiil is a god linked to lightning, serpents, and fertility. He is often shown with a serpent leg and a torch emerging from his forehead, symbolizing his power over life-giving rain and agricultural fertility.

*This is only a brief introduction to some of the suspected deities within Mayan culture. There are countless more gods/goddesses, and the ones listed here are a lot more complex than described.
